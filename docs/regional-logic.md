# Regional logic — Gulf + Middle East

How to scope analysis: when to stay in the Gulf core, when to extend, and what to redirect elsewhere.

## Gulf core (default scope)

Always-in-scope:

- **GCC states:** Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman.
- **Iran:** state, IRGC-affiliated, and private commercial actors — distinguish them.
- **Iraq:** federal government, Kurdistan Regional Government (KRG), Iraq oil flows, Central Bank of Iraq dollar auctions.
- **Hormuz** as the dominant maritime chokepoint.

If the question stays inside this core, extension is unnecessary.

## Conditional extension

Extend only when the extension changes the mechanism, exposure, leverage, or decision. Do not extend for decoration.

### Bab-el-Mandeb / Red Sea / Suez approaches

Include when:
- Houthi attacks or maritime security disruption is the primary driver.
- Shipping diversion (Cape of Good Hope routing) affects the user's exposure.
- Egyptian Suez Canal Authority transit data is relevant.
- LNG flows from Qatar to Europe are affected by Red Sea routing decisions.

### Levant (Lebanon, Syria, Jordan)

Include when:
- A financial flow runs through Lebanese banking (especially historical Iran-Hezbollah financing routes).
- Syrian sanctions exposure or post-2024 transition dynamics affect a counterparty.
- Jordanian transit, banking, or labor flows matter to the question.

Do not include for general Levantine political commentary.

### Israel and Israel-Gulf normalization

Include when:
- The question explicitly involves Abraham Accords-related flows, normalization, or counter-normalization.
- An Israeli counterparty, fund, or company has Gulf exposure.
- A regional security dynamic (Israel-Iran direct exchange, IRGC proxy activity) affects the user's risk.

Do not include for Israel-Palestine internal political detail; that requires specialist sources.

### Turkey

Include when:
- Turkey is a flow conduit for Iran sanctions evasion (historical: gold, banking, oil).
- Turkish KRG oil pipeline (Iraq-Türkiye Pipeline) is relevant.
- Turkish-Qatari financial relationships affect the question.

Do not include for general Turkey political analysis.

### Russia, China, India, US

Include when these external actors materially affect the Gulf flow:
- Russia: oil-market coordination with Saudi (OPEC+), parallel sanctions and dark-fleet patterns.
- China: largest buyer of Iranian sanctioned oil; Belt and Road exposure to Gulf ports; sovereign wealth co-investment.
- India: significant Iranian oil buyer historically; Chabahar port exposure.
- US: OFAC designations, Fifth Fleet posture, US-Iran negotiation status.
- EU: secondary sanctions exposure, EU-Gulf trade and energy.

## Out of scope — redirect

If the question is primarily about:

- **North Africa** (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Libya beyond oil-flow exposure to Gulf) → redirect to a Maghreb specialist resource.
- **Central Asia + Caspian** (Iran-Caspian flows, Iraq-Turkmenistan gas, Russia-Iran-Azerbaijan tri-junction) → cross-reference the Central Asia + Caspian Hybrid Intelligence Skill.
- **Israel-Palestine internal politics** → use Israel-Palestine specialist sources.
- **South Asia** (Afghanistan, Pakistan internal) beyond Iranian-Pakistani border or Chabahar port flows → out of scope.
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** beyond UAE/Saudi sovereign-wealth or Gulf-state diplomatic engagement → out of scope.

When out of scope, say so briefly and either redirect or decline to extend.

## Iran actor distinctions

The single most common analytical failure in Gulf + Iran analysis is conflating three distinct Iran actor categories. Always distinguish:

### Iran-state

- Government of Iran, ministries, Central Bank of Iran (CBI), state-owned enterprises (NIOC, NIDC, NPC).
- Subject to comprehensive US sanctions; subject to EU/UK measures with category variation.
- Operational implication: any direct exposure is a high-severity sanctions and AML concern.

### IRGC-affiliated

- IRGC, Quds Force, IRGC-controlled commercial networks (Khatam al-Anbiya construction headquarters, Bonyad Mostazafan-linked entities historically), designated Iranian proxies (Hezbollah, certain Iraqi militia commercial networks, Houthi commercial elements).
- IRGC is designated as a Foreign Terrorist Organization by the US (April 2019).
- Operational implication: any exposure carries terrorism-financing risk in addition to sanctions risk.

### Iran-private commercial

- Private Iranian firms without IRGC control or specific sanctions designation.
- Often family conglomerates operating in the bonyad-adjacent commercial space.
- May still be subject to comprehensive Iran sanctions depending on jurisdiction (US comprehensive embargo applies; EU narrower).
- Operational implication: sanctions exposure depends on jurisdiction; not all Iranian commercial activity is IRGC-linked.

For any Iran-related claim, name which category applies, or label as `Unknown — requires entity-level verification`.

## GCC actor distinctions

Within the GCC, distinguish:

- **State** (royal family, government ministries, central bank).
- **Sovereign wealth** (PIF, ADIA, Mubadala, QIA, KIA, Investcorp) — each with different mandates and governance.
- **Royal-family commercial** (members of ruling families with private commercial holdings, often opaque).
- **Private commercial** (independent merchant families, modern entrepreneurs).

The four often overlap, but they have different incentive structures and different exposure profiles. Avoid undifferentiated "Gulf money" framing.
