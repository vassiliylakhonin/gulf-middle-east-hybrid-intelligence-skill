## Signal

Houthi forces, operating under Iran-affiliated support, have sustained attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Bab-el-Mandeb strait since late 2023. The attacks have not been resolved by multinational naval presence (Operation Prosperity Guardian / EU Operation Aspides) or by the IDF campaign in Gaza. As of the date of this signal, major carriers have not resumed standard Red Sea routing at pre-disruption volumes; the Cape of Good Hope rerouting adds approximately 10–14 days to Asia-Europe transits. War-risk premiums for vessels transiting Bab-el-Mandeb remain materially elevated above pre-disruption levels.

**EVIDENCE ACCESS LIMITED: no live verification performed in this environment. Verify current routing volumes (Suez Canal Authority, IEA, Kpler) and war-risk premium levels (Lloyd's of London market, JWC) before operational use.**

## Why it matters

The Red Sea disruption is not a short-cycle event that self-corrects when the news cycle moves on. It has been sustained for over 18 months and has restructured Asia-Europe shipping economics. The strategic question for planners is not "when does it end" but "what does the cost structure look like if it does not end in the planning horizon." For energy traders and LNG buyers, Suez is a critical LNG artery; rerouting LNG adds cost and schedule uncertainty on top of commodity price exposure.

## Decision question

For a shipping insurer, energy buyer, container shipper, or supply-chain operator: should the Cape rerouting be priced as the new base case for 2026–2027 planning, or as a temporary deviation from a Suez baseline that will be restored?

## Mechanism

**Attack → rerouting → cost and schedule transmission:**
1. Houthi attacks on commercial vessels create physical and insurance risk for Bab-el-Mandeb transits.
2. Major carriers divert via the Cape of Good Hope rather than absorb war-risk premium and crew-safety exposure.
3. Cape rerouting adds ~10–14 days and significant fuel cost per voyage on Asia-Europe routes.
4. War-risk insurance premiums for vessels that do transit Bab-el-Mandeb remain elevated; some vessels and flag states are excluded from standard coverage, requiring specialist markets.
5. LNG-specific: Qatar, the UAE, and Egypt are significant LNG exporters whose cargoes previously transited Suez to Europe. Cape rerouting compresses LNG shipping capacity and adds to delivered-cost variability.
6. Hormuz-adjacent: the Red Sea disruption alone does not close Hormuz, but an escalation scenario that draws in Iran directly — rather than through the Houthi proxy layer — would stack a Hormuz closure risk on top of the Bab-el-Mandeb disruption.

**Second-order effects:**
- Egyptian Suez Canal Authority revenues have dropped materially; Egypt's fiscal position, already stressed, weakens further, affecting regional stability.
- Insurance market repricing has created a two-tier system: vessels with adequate war-risk cover transiting at elevated cost, vs. vessels that cannot obtain cover and avoid the route entirely.
- Spare shipping capacity absorbed by Cape rerouting has raised spot freight rates globally, with knock-on effects on non-Red-Sea supply chains.

## Quick assessment

- **Fact (Plausible — requires verification against current IEA/Kpler data):** Suez Canal transit volumes for container shipping remain significantly below pre-disruption levels; the majority of major carriers have maintained Cape rerouting as their standard lane.
- **Assessment (Judgment):** The structural cause — IRGC-backed Houthi capability to threaten commercial vessels — is unlikely to be removed within a 12-month planning horizon absent a Gaza ceasefire that includes a verified Houthi stand-down agreement, or a US-Iran deal that constrains IRGC weapons transfers. Neither is established as of the signal date.
- **Assumption:** Houthi capabilities remain sufficient to threaten vessels; naval escort operations do not provide comprehensive cover.
- **Unknown:** Whether an Iran-Houthi de-escalation agreement tied to a Gaza ceasefire is technically achievable and would be honored. This is the dominant unknown.
- **Main risk:** Planning around a Suez restoration that does not materialize, while competitors have already reconfigured supply chains and freight contracts for Cape routing.

## Who gains leverage

- Cape rerouting shipping lines: higher freight rates, more voyage days billed, competitive advantage over peers with Red Sea exposure.
- War-risk insurance specialists who can price the market correctly at current premium levels.
- LNG exporters in non-Red-Sea positions (US LNG, Norwegian, Australian) who are not route-constrained.
- Saudi Arabia and UAE as alternative oil-loading points for buyers who want to avoid Iranian-adjacency questions.

## Who loses leverage

- Suez-dependent exporters (Egypt, transit revenues).
- Container shippers and LNG buyers with fixed delivery schedules priced on pre-disruption transit times.
- Industrial buyers with lean-inventory supply chains that assumed predictable transit times.
- Insurers who have written war-risk cover at pre-disruption rates.

## Scenarios

- **Baseline — sustained disruption:** Houthi attacks continue at current tempo; no Gaza ceasefire with credible Houthi stand-down. Cape rerouting remains the majority carrier behavior through end-2026. War-risk premiums stay elevated. Plan this case.
- **De-escalation via Gaza ceasefire:** A Gaza ceasefire includes a verifiable Houthi stand-down commitment. Carriers gradually test Bab-el-Mandeb transit. War-risk premiums compress over 3–6 months. LNG and container shipping economics return toward a Suez baseline. This is the upside case — do not treat it as the planning base unless a ceasefire with Houthi terms is confirmed.
- **Escalation — Iran direct involvement:** A US strike on Iran or a Houthi miscalculation that draws in Iran directly. Hormuz threat activates simultaneously with Bab-el-Mandeb. Oil price corridor expands sharply; LNG routing options narrow. This is the tail scenario but not implausible given the IRGC-Houthi link.
- **Fragmented equilibrium:** Partial de-escalation — some carriers return to Suez, most do not; war-risk premiums fall moderately but not to pre-disruption levels. Two-tier routing persists: vessels with state-level risk tolerance (Chinese carriers, some Gulf-linked carriers) transit; Western carriers stay on Cape routes.

## Watch next

- **Suez Canal transit volumes** — Suez Canal Authority monthly traffic data; IEA Monthly Oil Market Report shipping section; Kpler or TankerTrackers cargo flow data. A sustained volume recovery above pre-disruption norms is the primary de-escalation signal.
- **War-risk premium levels at Bab-el-Mandeb** — JWC (Joint War Committee) area designations and market premium levels; Lloyd's Market Association bulletins. If premiums compress below the threshold where Cape rerouting is cost-competitive, carrier behavior will shift.
- **Gaza ceasefire negotiations and Houthi terms** — Any ceasefire that does not include a Houthi weapons stand-down and verification mechanism is unlikely to resolve the shipping disruption; watch specifically for Houthi-named terms in ceasefire frameworks.
- **IRGC-Houthi weapons transfer interdictions** — US Navy and allied interdictions of IRGC weapons shipments to Yemen (US CENTCOM statements, UN Panel of Experts reports). Sustained interdiction at scale would degrade Houthi long-range capability over time.
- **US-Iran diplomatic signal** — Any US-Iran engagement that includes a Houthi stand-down as a term of discussion changes the disruption trajectory materially.
