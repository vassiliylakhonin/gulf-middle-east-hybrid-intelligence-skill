## Signal

The US-Iran nuclear file is in one of its structurally most uncertain states: the JCPOA is not in force, the snapback mechanism under UN Security Council Resolution 2231 has been activated (European powers triggered snapback in 2022, restoring pre-JCPOA UN sanctions), Iran's uranium enrichment level has advanced significantly beyond JCPOA limits (enrichment to 60%+ and beyond, with stockpiles assessed by the IAEA), and US-Iran direct and indirect negotiations have occurred without producing a formal agreement. Any near-term outcome — from a new nuclear deal with sanctions relief, to a military strike on Iranian nuclear facilities, to a prolonged no-deal stalemate — remains structurally plausible given the current configuration.

## Why it matters

The US-Iran nuclear file is the single highest-leverage uncertainty for the Gulf + Middle East risk landscape:
- A nuclear deal with sanctions relief would materially change: OFAC Iran sanctions architecture, Iranian oil export volumes and GCC market-share dynamics, Iranian access to the financial system, and GCC states' security posture.
- A military strike on Iranian nuclear facilities would trigger: Iranian retaliation options (Hormuz closure threat, IRGC proxy activation), energy market disruption (Brent spike to $100+), shipping disruption escalation beyond the current Houthi-Red Sea framework, and potentially a regional military confrontation.
- A prolonged stalemate (current trajectory) means continued OFAC comprehensive sanctions, Iran sanctions circumvention through GCC hubs, IRGC-affiliated network pressure, and continued uncertainty premium in energy and shipping markets.

Planning on any single outcome from this file is a methodological error. The planning discipline is scenario-based, not forecast-based.

## Mechanism

**Deal scenario — sanctions relief pathway:**
1. A US-Iran nuclear agreement reduces Iranian uranium enrichment level to JCPOA or near-JCPOA limits.
2. OFAC issues General Licenses and SDN removals under a new Iran deal waiver framework; EU and UK follow.
3. Iranian oil exports rise (potentially 1–1.5 mbd incremental in 12–18 months — verify against EIA/IEA estimates at the time).
4. OPEC+ must absorb or offset Iranian volume increase; Saudi Arabia and the UAE face market-share competition.
5. Iranian entities previously on the SDN list receive relief; GCC-hub banking de-risk calculus shifts.
6. IRGC-designated entities may not receive relief; entity-level verification still required; deal terms must be read specifically.

**Strike scenario — military escalation pathway:**
1. US (and/or Israeli) military strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities.
2. Iran activates retaliation options: (a) Hormuz closure threat / mine-laying; (b) IRGC proxy attacks on Saudi/UAE energy infrastructure; (c) Houthi escalation in Red Sea beyond current scope.
3. Oil price spikes sharply (Brent to $100–$130 range under partial Hormuz disruption; higher under full closure — see Hormuz signal).
4. OFAC Iran sanctions architecture tightens further; no relief scenario.
5. GCC states face security pressure and potential for regional military conflict.

**Stalemate scenario — no deal, no strike:**
1. Current trajectory: comprehensive OFAC sanctions continue; Iranian enrichment advances; periodic diplomatic contacts without agreement.
2. Iran continues oil exports through sanctions circumvention (GCC hubs, Chinese buyers at discount, dark-fleet).
3. IRGC-affiliated networks continue operating; compliance risk for GCC-hub financial institutions persists.
4. Low-intensity proxy activity (Houthi, Iraqi Shia militia) continues; no major escalation.
5. Uncertainty premium persists in energy and shipping markets.

## Quick assessment

- **Fact (Verified — IAEA public reporting):** Iranian uranium enrichment has advanced significantly beyond JCPOA limits. IAEA safeguards continuity has been affected by Iranian restrictions on inspector access. These are documented facts in IAEA Director General reports.
- **Assessment (Judgment):** The structural distance between Iranian enrichment levels and a usable nuclear weapon is now smaller than at any point since the 1979 revolution. This creates both increased military-option urgency (especially for Israel) and increased Iranian leverage in any negotiation.
- **Assumption:** Iran's leadership has not yet made a final decision to cross the nuclear-weapons threshold; deterrence and negotiating leverage, not weapons development, remain the primary drivers of the enrichment program. This assumption is contested.
- **Unknown:** Iranian supreme leadership intent; US administration's red lines and willingness to use military force; whether any indirect talks have produced near-term deal parameters.
- **Main risk:** Making Iran-adjacent operational or financial decisions anchored on one scenario (deal, strike, or stalemate) when the structural uncertainty warrants scenario-based planning.

## Who gains leverage

- Iran: advanced enrichment creates both a deterrent and a negotiating chip. Paradoxically, higher enrichment can increase leverage in deal negotiations.
- Israel: if military option is exercised, Israel gains a window of nuclear facility degradation — at the cost of potential regional escalation.
- GCC states (deal scenario): sanctions relief and Iranian reintegration would allow GCC states to reposition as regional economic integrators rather than adversaries.

## Who loses leverage

- US: the longer a deal is delayed, the more Iranian enrichment advances, narrowing the military option's effectiveness window.
- Western energy companies: deal scenario could open Iranian energy sector; stalemate or strike scenario closes it indefinitely.
- GCC-hub financial institutions: in all scenarios, Iran-related compliance exposure persists; the stalemate scenario means continued, costly vigilance.

## Scenarios

- **Scenario A — Nuclear deal (Low probability, High impact):** A new US-Iran nuclear agreement produces OFAC General License issuances and partial SDN removals. Iranian oil exports rise. Banking de-risking for Iran-adjacent entities partially reverses. Key trigger: formal US-Iran agreement announcement with OFAC guidance.
- **Scenario B — Military strike (Low-to-Moderate probability, Very High impact):** Israeli and/or US military strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities. Iranian retaliation. Regional military escalation risk. Energy market disruption. Trigger: credible strike preparation signals (US carrier deployment posture, Israeli air-to-air refuelling exercises, UN IAEA emergency session).
- **Scenario C — Prolonged stalemate (High probability, Moderate impact):** No deal, no strike. Current dynamics continue. OFAC sanctions remain comprehensive. Iranian oil flows through circumvention channels. GCC-hub compliance pressure persists. This is the base case for planning.
- **Scenario D — Iranian nuclear breakout (Very Low probability, Extreme impact):** Iran crosses the nuclear-weapons threshold. US and allied response triggers the most severe sanctions and military options. Extreme tail scenario; not the planning base.

## Watch next

- **IAEA Director General reports** — https://www.iaea.org/newscenter/pressreleases — quarterly updates on Iranian enrichment levels and inspector access. Any IAEA Board of Governors emergency meeting is a significant escalation signal.
- **US OFAC Iran sanctions actions** — https://ofac.treasury.gov/recent-actions — new designations, General License issuances, or revocations. A GL with broad Iran relief is the operative deal signal.
- **US State Department and Iranian Foreign Ministry statements on nuclear talks** — direct diplomatic signal; look for: named venue, named parties, named topics; absence of named specifics means nothing material is imminent.

- **UN Security Council Iran snapback status** — if a deal is reached, snapback restoration of pre-JCPOA UN sanctions would need to be addressed. UNSC P5 dynamics on Iran are a leading indicator of deal viability.
- **Brent crude price movement on Iran headlines** — market's implicit probability assessment of deal vs. strike scenarios. A sharp Brent spike on an Iran headline (not driven by OPEC+ or Hormuz) indicates the market is pricing a strike scenario.
