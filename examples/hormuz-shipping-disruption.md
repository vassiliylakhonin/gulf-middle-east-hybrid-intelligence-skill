# Hormuz shipping disruption — exposure framing for a shipping insurer

**Question:** How should a shipping insurer with significant Hormuz-transiting tanker book frame and price war-risk exposure during a period of elevated US-Iran tension and sporadic incident activity?

## Bottom line

Treat Hormuz exposure as a risk corridor, not a point estimate. Premium structure should reflect the gap between the baseline (sporadic incidents, no closure) and the upside (sustained closure or named seizure event), with explicit exposure-aggregation limits to manage clash risk. Anchoring premium on either the median-recent or the worst-recent quarter underprices the dispersion.

## Primary driver

## Why now

Sustained pattern of Iranian harassment and US Fifth Fleet posture changes in the Gulf creates a period where individual-incident war-risk premiums adjust quickly but the underlying outcome dispersion is wider than premiums fully reflect.

## Mechanism

- Iranian leverage over Hormuz operates through three channels: (a) direct harassment (boardings, GPS spoofing, vessel seizures); (b) Houthi proxy attacks in Bab-el-Mandeb extending the threat geography; (c) credible threat of full or partial closure under escalation scenarios.
- War-risk insurance premiums move quickly on incidents but lag on structural escalation. Underwriting during a "no incident this week" lull can systematically misprice the corridor.
- Exposure aggregation across an insurer's book transmits incident risk into a clash-risk concentration: a single closure event affects a large share of insured tonnage simultaneously.

## Exposure map

- **Highest exposure:** crude tankers with Persian Gulf loading and US/EU discharge, exposed to both Hormuz and downstream re-routing decisions.
- **Conditional exposure:** LNG carriers from Qatar with European discharge — directly Hormuz-dependent and routing-dependent on Bab-el-Mandeb.
- **Reduced exposure:** intra-Gulf coastal trade, tankers with Asian discharge that can route via Cape if needed.
- **Indirect exposure:** P&I clubs, hull and machinery underwriters, freight-rate forward contracts.

## Actor incentives and leverage

- **Iran:** uses Hormuz harassment as a calibrated escalation tool, not a closure objective. Closure damages Iranian oil revenue (Iran transits Hormuz too); incidents short of closure are the dominant pattern.
- **GCC states:** strong incentive to keep Hormuz open; Saudi Arabia and UAE have built (limited) bypass pipeline capacity but cannot fully replace Hormuz transit volume.
- **US Fifth Fleet:** demonstrated willingness to escort and protect tonnage; not unlimited capacity.
- **Shipping lines and tanker owners:** willing to pay risk premium for continued transit; will divert (Cape of Good Hope for Bab-el-Mandeb-affected routes; reduced Hormuz transit harder to substitute).
- **War-risk insurance market:** competitive pressure on premiums during quiet periods; sharp re-pricing on named incidents.

## Role-based implications (shipping insurer)

1. **Premium structure as corridor, not point:** quote against a corridor reflecting both baseline (sporadic incidents) and upside (sustained closure or major named incident) probabilities. Avoid premium structures anchored solely on most recent quarter incident frequency.
2. **Exposure aggregation limits:** set per-corridor and per-day aggregation caps to manage clash risk on a closure event affecting many insured vessels simultaneously.
3. **Underwriting discipline by vessel class:** crude tankers vs. LNG carriers vs. product tankers carry different ownership, insurance market, and counterparty profiles. Underwrite by class with different escalation triggers.
4. **Incident-trigger premium adjustments:** define pre-authorized premium adjustments for named trigger events (vessel seizure >24 hours, missile/drone attack on a vessel in transit, US Fifth Fleet posture change). Faster adjustment beats waiting for market consensus.
5. **Reinsurance coordination:** review treaty terms for clash-event definitions and aggregation. Hormuz-corridor wording matters more than headline limit.
6. **Cargo provenance overlay:** dark-fleet vessels carrying sanctioned-origin cargo represent a sanctions-cum-war-risk dual exposure; underwriting should screen for this rather than treat war-risk in isolation.

## Trigger points

- Named vessel seizure or boarding incident in the Strait or Gulf, with detention >24 hours.
- Missile or drone attack on a tanker in transit (Hormuz or Bab-el-Mandeb).
- US Fifth Fleet change in escort posture or convoy operations.
- Iranian official statement on Hormuz transit (rare but historically significant).
- Houthi targeting pattern shift (extending range, new vessel-class targeting).
- War-risk insurance market premium step-change indicating market consensus update.
- Major shipping line announcement of routing change (Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM, tanker pool operators).

## Unknowns

- Iranian decision threshold for moving from harassment to closure — appears high but is not directly observable.
- US Fifth Fleet escort capacity ceiling under sustained operations.
- Houthi anti-ship capability evolution and supply chain.
- Insurance-market clash-risk aggregation transparency across the wider P&I and war-risk market.

## Confidence

**Moderate.** The mechanism, actor-incentive structure, and underwriting implications are well-established. The specific dispersion of outcomes and the timing of incident triggers are inherently uncertain.

## What would change this judgment

- Sustained Hormuz transit reduction (>20% week-on-week) for two consecutive weeks → closure scenario probability rises sharply; premium structure must shift from corridor to closure-priced.
- Major incident (tanker loss or significant detention) → immediate premium step-change and aggregation review.
- US-Iran negotiation breakthrough with credible de-escalation framework → corridor narrows back toward baseline; premium pressure downward.
- New Houthi capability demonstration extending threat geography (e.g., further into Indian Ocean) → reroute economics shift further toward Cape, with different exposure profile.
