# Iran sanctions routing exposure — European refiner sourcing Gulf crude

**Question:** What Iran sanctions adjacency risk does a European refiner face when sourcing crude from Gulf trading hubs (UAE, Oman) via intermediary trading houses?
**Decision:** Whether to maintain, deepen due diligence on, or restructure trading-house counterparty relationships over the next 6–12 months.
**Audience:** Refinery sanctions compliance and trade-finance.
**Time horizon:** Near-term (6–12 months).
**Evidence mode:** `reasoning-only`.
**Limitation note:** This is a `reasoning-only` illustrative example. It does not cite live OFAC designations, specific entities, vessels, or enforcement actions. Operational sanctions decisions require primary OFAC SDN List checks, qualified compliance review, and entity-level verification.

---

## Bottom line

Adjacency risk concentrates not in the GCC trading hub itself but in the intermediary trading-house layer: free-zone entities with thin operational footprint, opaque beneficial ownership, and short documentary distance to Iranian-origin or sanctioned-blend cargoes. The refiner's binding control point is provenance documentation depth on the cargo, not the GCC source-jurisdiction label.

## Scope and evidence mode

`reasoning-only`. No live source verification. Structural reasoning about risk transmission patterns; not an entity-level assessment.

## Primary driver

`Primary driver is: documentary opacity at the intermediary trading-house layer that allows sanctioned-origin crude to enter a GCC-labeled cargo flow.`

## Mechanism

- Iranian-origin or sanctioned-blend crude moves via dark-fleet vessels through STS transfers in permissive zones (Persian Gulf, eastern Mediterranean).
- Cargo is re-papered as a Gulf-origin or "blend" product through trading-house documentation.
- A free-zone trading entity (often UAE, occasionally Oman or Singapore) with shallow operational footprint serves as the documentary counterparty to the European refiner.
- The refiner pays a Gulf-jurisdiction entity, receives a Gulf-labeled cargo, and books a non-Iranian-origin transaction.
- Sanctions exposure transmits through: (a) OFAC enforcement reach to the trading-house entity if sanctioned-origin crude is traced to the cargo; (b) correspondent-banking risk on the USD payment leg; (c) reputational and regulatory risk on disclosure if the cargo is later identified.

## Exposure map

- **Concentrated at:** the trading-house counterparty layer, especially free-zone entities incorporated within the past 24 months with no website, no audited financials, and beneficial owners not transparently disclosed.
- **Less exposed:** direct refinery-to-NOC arrangements with GCC national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, KPC, QatarEnergy) where provenance is institutionally established.
- **Highest residual risk:** "spot" cargoes routed through multi-counterparty trading chains where the trading-house counterparty changed within the last 6 months.

## Actor incentives and leverage

- **Iran-state and IRGC-affiliated commercial networks:** maximize sanctioned-oil revenue by widening the buyer pool and obscuring provenance. Strong incentive to support documentary cover at the trading-house layer.
- **GCC trading-house entities:** range from established merchants (low-risk) to newly incorporated free-zone shells (high-risk). Reputational and enforcement risk for established merchants creates self-screening incentive; newly incorporated shells have weak self-screening incentive.
- **Western refiner:** wants reliable Gulf-origin crude supply at competitive pricing; sanctions enforcement risk and reputational exposure create strong incentive for provenance discipline if internalized.
- **OFAC:** has demonstrated willingness to enforce against trading-house facilitators and downstream buyers; enforcement against major Western refiners would be high-impact but politically costly.
- **Correspondent banks:** strong incentive to de-risk on payment flows tied to suspect Gulf trading-house counterparties; this transmits enforcement pressure to the refiner via banking access.

## Role-based implications (refiner sanctions compliance and trade finance)

1. **Trading-house counterparty diligence:** apply enhanced due diligence to trading-house counterparties incorporated in free zones within the last 24 months, with thin operational footprint or opaque beneficial ownership. Tier counterparties: tier 1 (established Gulf merchant houses with multi-decade footprint), tier 2 (mid-tier with verifiable footprint), tier 3 (recent free-zone entities — require enhanced provenance for any cargo).
2. **Cargo provenance documentation:** require documentation depth proportionate to counterparty tier. For tier 3 counterparties: certificate of origin, vessel-history summary, declaration of no STS transfers in restricted zones, and right to audit cargo provenance.
3. **Vessel-history checks:** for any spot cargo, screen the carrying vessel against OFAC vessel designations and against AIS-derived dark-fleet patterns (use a maritime intelligence vendor; do not rely on spot AIS).
4. **Banking-side coordination:** treat correspondent-bank de-risking signals as a leading indicator. If the refiner's Gulf-leg correspondent has tightened KYC on a trading-house counterparty, treat it as a posture-change trigger.
5. **Documentary thresholds:** define triggers for moving a counterparty between tiers based on observable signals (incorporation age, BO transparency, audit history, prior counterparty enforcement actions).

## Trigger points

- OFAC SDN List addition naming a Gulf-jurisdiction trading-house entity.
- OFAC enforcement settlement against a refiner or trading house involving Iranian-origin cargo cover.
- New OFAC FAQ guidance on "blend" or "origin" representations.
- EU Council adopting parallel measures on Iranian-origin cargo cover (lower probability, higher impact for European refiners).
- Correspondent bank exiting a payment-rail relationship with a counterparty.
- Vessel designations that touch the refiner's counterparty's recent shipping history.

## Unknowns

- Specific current entity-level designation status — must be verified against the live SDN List for any operational decision.
- Beneficial ownership structures of any specific trading-house counterparty — requires registry data and entity-level diligence.
- Current OFAC enforcement queue and prioritization on this transmission channel.
- Whether the EU is moving toward parallel enforcement (regulatory rather than market-driven divergence).

## Confidence

**Moderate.** The risk-transmission mechanism is well-established and consistent with public OFAC enforcement patterns. Specific entity-level claims require source-backed verification.

## What would change this judgment

- A major OFAC enforcement action against a Western refiner involving GCC trading-house cover → counterparty restructuring becomes urgent, not optional.
- EU Council adoption of enforcement measures parallel to OFAC on Iranian-origin cargo → European-jurisdiction risk rises sharply.
- US-Iran nuclear file movement that leads to material sanctions easing → trading-house counterparty risk declines, but provenance discipline should remain (snapback risk).
- Major correspondent-bank exit from Gulf trading-house payment flows → operational disruption risk rises independently of the sanctions-enforcement risk.
